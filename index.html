Hi, I'm Christina!

I am a Postdoctoral Researcher at the University of British Columbia in the School of Regional and Community Planning (SCARP) and a Visiting Scientist at the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health.

Broadly speaking, I apply geophysical methods and state-of-the-art data science to address policy-relevant questions on climate change, with applications spanning glaciology, socio-economic sciences, and public and planetary health.

Background

Education, Research, and Industry Experience

My academic background is in geophysics, applied mathematics, and data science, with studies in Canada, Austria, and Germany. I hold a PhD in Geophysics from the Department of Earth, Ocean and Atmospheric Sciences at the University of British Columbia and currently conduct research at the School of Regional and Community Planning (SCARP) and the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health.

Before and during my PhD, I gained interdisciplinary research experience across several roles, including as a research assistant at the Urban Predictive Analytics Lab at the University of British Columbia, through the Portfolio Management Program at the Research Institute for Capital Markets in Austria, and as a research assistant at the Institute for Supply Chain Management at the University of St. Gallen.

In addition, I have held a range of industry positions in data science and machine learning consulting, asset management, and private banking across England, Switzerland, Latvia, Norway, and Austria, and I have also worked in sustainable development, resilience, and food security with the German Development Cooperation (GIZ) in Madagascar.

Outreach and Policy Engagement

I am actively involved in outreach, science communication, and policy engagement, with a focus on translating climate change research, including my own, into actionable insights. I have presented my work at high-level international conferences attended by policymakers, including as an Early Career Scientist (ECS) volunteer for the International Cryosphere Climate Initiative (ICCI) at COP29 in Azerbaijan and at the High-Level Conference on Glacier Preservation in Tajikistan. I have also co-organized the Graduate Climate Conference twice, an interdisciplinary three-day event that brought together 120 international graduate students in climate science.

Beyond this, I have explored creative approaches to science communication by collaborating with composers to create music inspired by my research on glacier melt, which was performed in public concerts in Vancouver. In addition, I co-created a 15-minute documentary on gender inequality in Madagascar, for which I wrote large parts of the script in French, directed several sections, and oversaw the editing process.

Research

The overarching goal of my research is to develop interdisciplinary quantitative methodologies that advance our understanding of policy-relevant environmental issues in the age of climate change and drive tangible real-world impact. At its core, my research creates synergies across disciplines by synthesizing, transferring and integrating concepts from geophysics, data science, machine learning, and climate and environmental sciences in innovative ways.

A core focus of my research is on climate downscaling—both statistical and dynamical using regional climate models—to translate large-scale global climate signals into local-scale exposures for various applications.

Surface Energy Balance Glacier Modeling

My PhD research at the University of British Columbia focused on regional glacier evolution modeling using physics-based surface energy balance to simulate both historical and future glacier change in Western Canada. I developed a novel neural network- and remote sensing-based albedo model, investigated the effectiveness of dynamical downscaling for climate forcing, and assessed how different downscaling methods influence uncertainties in future glacier projections from global climate models.

Infrastructure Planning in Canada

At the Urban Predictive Analytics Lab at the University of British Columbia, I contribute to the Virtual Infrastructure Systems Simulation (VISION) project, a system-of-systems modeling framework for predicting future infrastructure service demand across Canada at different spatial and temporal scales under different socioeconomic pathways, with special focus on vulnerable populations. In particular, I develop new methodologies and implement high-resolution population and transport models.

Climate-Health Associations in Madagascar

At the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, I support the Climate-Smart Public Health initiative by developing human-centered climate downscaling methods to bridge scale mismatches between global climate datasets and local clinical health data in Madagascar. I also lead a project to generate high-resolution air quality estimates and quantify wildfire smoke contributions using deep learning models that integrate ground-based sensors, remote sensing, and chemical transport simulations.
